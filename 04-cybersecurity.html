02 · Know Your Threats

AI-Powered Attacks: What You're Actually Facing

These are not theoretical. Every single one of these attacks is being used right now.

⚡ Critical

Deepfake Voice & Video Fraud

AI generates convincing fake audio and video of real people — your CEO, your bank, your family member — to trick you into taking action.

⚠️ Real case: A company transferred $25 million after a video call with a "senior executive" who was entirely AI-generated. The employee heard the voice, saw the face — and was completely deceived.
✅ Defence: Establish a verbal codeword for any financial request. Call back on a known number — never the one provided in the suspicious message.
⚡ Critical

AI-Personalised Phishing (Spear Phishing)

AI scrapes your social media, LinkedIn, email leaks, and public data to craft a phishing message that knows your name, your boss's name, your current project, and mimics writing styles you trust.

⚠️ Africa example: Fraudsters scraping M-Pesa and mobile money platform data to send hyper-personalised "fraud alerts" in the victim's own transaction language, harvesting credentials.
✅ Defence: Verify any unexpected request through a separate channel. If it creates urgency — that's the attack. Slow down.
🔴 High

AI-Generated Malware

Attackers use AI to generate novel malware variants faster than security tools can update. Code that would have taken skilled hackers weeks to write now takes minutes to generate.

⚠️ Impact: Small organisations without enterprise security software are particularly vulnerable — the malware is too new for signature-based detection to catch it.
✅ Defence: Behaviour-based endpoint security (not just signature-based). Regular patching. Staff training to not open unexpected attachments.
🔴 High

Social Engineering at Scale

AI chatbots and voice bots can run thousands of simultaneous scam conversations — WhatsApp romance scams, "investment opportunity" fraud, fake customer service — without a human scammer involved.

⚠️ Africa context: WhatsApp is the primary communication channel for business in much of Africa — making it the primary attack surface. An AI bot can maintain 500 "relationships" simultaneously.
✅ Defence: If an online relationship moves quickly to financial requests, assume fraud. Video call verification. Tell someone you trust before sending any money.
🟡 Medium

Data Poisoning

Attackers inject false or manipulated data into AI training sets to corrupt the model's outputs — causing AI systems to make biased, incorrect, or malicious decisions.

⚠️ Example: A healthcare AI trained on manipulated data begins recommending incorrect dosages for a specific demographic — causing harm that appears random and is extremely difficult to detect.
✅ Defence: Audit your AI training data sources. Use validated datasets. Regularly test AI outputs against known-good benchmarks.
🟡 Medium

Credential Stuffing & Password Cracking

AI can test millions of password combinations per second. Leaked databases from one platform become the attack toolkit for every other platform where you reused that password.

⚠️ Scale: Over 15 billion stolen credentials are in circulation online. If you use the same password across accounts, assume at least one has been compromised.
✅ Defence: Password manager + unique passwords + multi-factor authentication on everything critical. Non-negotiable.
03 · Personal Security

Your Personal Cybersecurity Checklist

Most breaches start with a person. You are both the weakest link and the strongest defence — depending on your habits.

Work through this checklist. Each item you check off meaningfully reduces your risk. Be honest.

🔑 Use a password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password, or similar)
Every account gets a unique, random, 20+ character password. You only remember one master password.
📱 Enable 2-Factor Authentication (2FA) on all critical accounts
Email, banking, social media, work tools. Use an authenticator app — not SMS if possible (SMS can be intercepted via SIM swap).
🔄 Keep devices and software updated
Security patches fix known vulnerabilities. An unpatched device is an open door. Enable automatic updates.
🧠 Learn to recognise phishing
Urgency + unexpected request + link or attachment = STOP. Verify through a different channel before clicking anything.
💾 Backup your critical data — 3-2-1 rule
3 copies, on 2 different media types, 1 offsite or in the cloud. Test your backups — an untested backup is not a backup.
📡 Avoid public WiFi for sensitive activity
Cafés, airports, hotels — attackers can intercept unencrypted traffic. Use a VPN or mobile data for banking and work.
🔒 Check if your email has been in a data breach
Visit haveibeenpwned.com — if your email appears, change passwords immediately on those services.
💬 Establish a verification protocol for financial requests
In your organisation: no financial transfer without a second independent verification. A phone call to a known number. A code word. Not just a reply to an email.

05 · Organisational Security

Building a Cyber-Resilient Organisation

Most African SMEs and NGOs operate with zero formal cybersecurity policy. This is not acceptable — and it's fixable.

🏢 The 5 Non-Negotiables for Any Organisation

1. Write a Cybersecurity Policy
Even one page: acceptable use, data handling, incident reporting, and password requirements. It signals that security is taken seriously — which alone changes behaviour.
2. Train Your People
95% of breaches involve human error. One hour of phishing awareness training per year dramatically reduces your risk. The weakest link is always the uninformed person.
3. Control Access — Principle of Least Privilege
Every employee should only have access to the data they need to do their job. When someone leaves, revoke access immediately. Shared passwords are banned.
4. Have an Incident Response Plan
When (not if) a breach happens: who do you call? What do you shut down first? Who informs clients? Having this decided before the crisis is the difference between recovery and catastrophe.
5. Regular Backups + Test Them
Ransomware encrypts your files and demands payment. Your only leverage is a backup that works. Test your restore process quarterly — not after you need it.
🧠

The Human Firewall — The Most Important Defence

No software can fully protect an organisation where people don't know the threats. The single highest-ROI investment in cybersecurity is training your people to recognise and report attacks. A team that spots phishing, questions unusual requests, and reports incidents quickly is more valuable than any technology tool.

06 · Deepfake Detection

How to Spot AI-Generated Content

Deepfakes are improving rapidly. But they still have tells — for now.

⚠️ Warning Signs in Video

  • Unnatural eye blinking (too frequent or too rare)
  • Blurry or misaligned hairline edges
  • Inconsistent lighting on face vs background
  • Mouth movements that don't perfectly sync with audio
  • Unusual skin texture — too smooth or pixelated under stress
  • Earrings, glasses, teeth that distort or flicker

⚠️ Warning Signs in Audio/Voice

  • Slightly robotic or overly consistent cadence
  • Background noise that's too clean or too consistent
  • Unnatural pauses or transitions between words
  • Breathing that sounds artificial
  • The "voice" refuses or struggles with unexpected questions
  • Creates urgency that prevents you from thinking clearly
⚡

The Verification Protocol — Always Use This

If someone contacts you with an urgent financial or security request — even if you can see and hear them: 1) End the call or session. 2) Call back on a number you already know. 3) Use your pre-agreed codeword or challenge question. 4) Never let urgency override verification. Real emergencies can wait 3 minutes for a callback. Fraud cannot — it relies on you not waiting.

Exercise 02 · Organisation

🏢 The 30-Minute Security Policy Sprint

If your organisation doesn't have a cybersecurity policy, use this session to draft one. It only needs to cover the basics — something is infinitely better than nothing.

  • Acceptable use of work devices and accounts
  • Password requirements (length, complexity, no sharing)
  • How to report a suspected security incident (who to call, first steps)
  • Data classification — what can be shared externally and what cannot
  • Consequences of policy violation
💡

AI can help you draft this! Use ChatGPT or Claude: "Draft a basic cybersecurity policy for a small NGO in Botswana with 15 employees. Keep it under 2 pages, plain English, covering: acceptable use, passwords, data handling, and incident reporting." — then review and adapt it to your context. This is the right way to use AI: it drafts, you judge.
