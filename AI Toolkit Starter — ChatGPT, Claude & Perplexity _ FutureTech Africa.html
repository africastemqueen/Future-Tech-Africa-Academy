04 · How These Actually Work

No Magic. No Mind-Reading.

Understanding this one idea removes 80% of AI anxiety.

All three tools are built on what's called a Large Language Model (LLM). In plain terms: they were trained on enormous amounts of text and learned the patterns of language — what words and ideas typically follow each other.

When you type a question, the AI isn't "looking you up" or "remembering your business" from some master file. It's generating a response based on patterns — and on whatever you've typed into that conversation.

That's the whole privacy question, really: what you type in is the information that exists about you in that system. Control the input, and you control your exposure.

06 · Setup — 10 Minutes Each

Getting Started Safely

Same basic process for all three. Do this once, properly, and you're set up for life.

  1. Go to the official site — chatgpt.com, claude.ai, or perplexity.ai. Avoid app-store copies or "AI chat" apps that aren't the real company — many are scams or data-harvesters wearing a costume.
  2. Sign up with a work or dedicated email — not your personal email if you can help it. This makes it easier to manage later if you upgrade to a business/team plan.
  3. Use a strong, unique password — or "Continue with Google/Apple" if you already use 2-factor authentication on that account.
  4. Go straight to Settings before you type anything — every tool has a "Data Controls" or "Privacy" section. This is Section 7 below — do it now, not later.
  5. Start with a low-stakes test — ask it to summarise a public news article or draft a generic email. Get a feel for it before using real client information.
07 · Safety & Privacy Settings

The Settings You Must Change — Do This, Not That

This is the section that turns "scared of AI" into "in control of AI." Takes under 10 minutes total. Each platform gets a clear list — what to switch on, and what to leave alone or avoid.

🤖 ChatGPT

Core fix: go to Settings → Data Controls. Turn OFF "Improve the model for everyone" — without this, your conversations may be reviewed and used to train future models, even on paid Plus.

✅ Do This
  • Turn off "Improve the model for everyone" before your first real chat
  • Use Temporary Chat for one-off sensitive questions — it doesn't save to history or memory
  • Review what ChatGPT has stored in Memory (Settings → Personalization) every few weeks and delete anything client-specific
  • Build Custom GPTs with placeholder data, then swap in real details only inside your own conversation
🚫 Don't Do This
  • Don't paste client contracts, ID numbers, or passwords — even "just to summarise"
  • Don't connect ChatGPT to your email or calendar (where offered) until you've read exactly what permissions it requests
  • Don't share a Custom GPT publicly with your business instructions baked into its files — files can sometimes be extracted
  • Don't assume Deep Research results are final — it cites sources, but always spot-check the key figures

🧠 Claude

Core fix: go to Settings → Privacy. By default, Claude does not use Free or Pro conversations for training — confirm the toggle is in the "do not use my data for training" position and you're set.

✅ Do This
  • Confirm the training toggle is OFF (it's the default — just verify, don't assume)
  • Set up a Project per client or programme so context and files stay separated and organised
  • Use Artifacts for anything you'll reuse — documents, slide outlines, code — so you get an editable file, not just chat text
  • If you connect Gmail/Drive/Slack via Connectors, review exactly which folders/scopes you're granting before approving
🚫 Don't Do This
  • Don't mix multiple clients' sensitive files inside one Project — create separate Projects
  • Don't grant Computer Use, Cowork, or Connector permissions to accounts that hold financial or banking access unless you've reviewed every permission requested
  • Don't rely on "30-day retention" as a reason to be careless — anonymise first regardless
  • Don't leave Extended Thinking on for simple tasks — it's slower and unnecessary for quick questions

🔍 Perplexity

Core fix: on the free plan, treat it like a public search engine — don't type anything you wouldn't type into Google. For real client/business research, Pro defaults to opting out of training.

✅ Do This
  • Use Perplexity for public, factual research — competitor news, market data, current events
  • Click through and verify at least one cited source before relying on an answer for a client
  • If using Comet browser, be aware it can read the page you're on — close sensitive tabs (banking, email) before activating agentic features
  • Use Spaces to organise research by topic so your "memory" stays relevant and useful
🚫 Don't Do This
  • Don't use the free tier for anything involving real client names, figures, or strategy — given the 2026 data-sharing allegations, treat free Perplexity as fully public
  • Don't let Comet's agentic features submit forms or make purchases unattended — review every action before it completes
  • Don't treat Model Council or Deep Research outputs as legal, financial, or medical advice — they synthesise public information, nothing more
⚡

The Golden Rule — Applies to ALL Three

Never type into any AI tool: client names paired with their financial details, ID/passport numbers, passwords, medical information, anything under an NDA, or anything you wouldn't be comfortable appearing in a screenshot one day. Anonymise first — replace "Acme Corp paid us BWP 450,000" with "Client A paid us [amount]" — then ask your question.

08 · Prompting Like a Pro

The Difference Between a Mediocre Answer and a Brilliant One

Same tool, same plan, wildly different output — the only variable is how you ask. This framework works on all three platforms.

R

Role

Tell the AI who it should "be" for this task — its expertise sets the frame for everything that follows.

"You are an experienced corporate trainer designing a workshop for senior government officials in Botswana."
T

Task

State exactly what you need — one clear deliverable, not five vague ones.

"Draft a 90-minute workshop outline on AI literacy for non-technical executives."
C

Context

Give the background that changes the answer — audience, constraints, prior attempts, what "good" looks like.

"The audience has never used AI tools before. They are senior, time-poor, and skeptical. Avoid jargon."
F

Format

Specify exactly how you want it structured — length, tone, headings, table vs prose.

"Output as a table: time block | activity | objective. Keep total under 400 words."
💡

The One Habit That Multiplies Everything Else

Don't accept the first answer — refine it. "Make this more formal," "shorten this by half," "rewrite this for a Setswana-speaking audience," "give me three alternative openings" — every follow-up is free and instant. Most people stop at draft one. Professionals iterate three or four times in the same conversation, because the AI remembers everything said so far in that chat.
