01 · Why Ethics Cannot Be Optional

The Stakes Have Never Been Higher

AI is making decisions about people's lives right now. Without ethical guardrails, it causes harm at scale.

77% of AI applications deployed globally contain at least one identified bias (MIT Media Lab)
~1% of global AI training data comes from African sources — for a continent of 1.4B people
0 African countries in the original G7 AI ethics frameworks — written without us, applied to us

This Is Not Abstract Philosophy

AI is currently making decisions about whether you get a loan, whether you're flagged at a border, whether your job application is shortlisted, whether your medical image gets reviewed, and whether you appear as a criminal suspect in a police database. In most of these cases, you will never know an AI was involved — or that it was wrong.

02 · The Framework

The 6 Principles of Responsible AI

These are not ideals. They are minimum requirements. If an AI system fails any of these, it should not be deployed.

01

Fairness — Do No Harm to Already Marginalised Groups

AI systems must not discriminate based on race, gender, age, disability, economic status, or location. Given that most AI is trained on Western data, fairness must be actively designed in — it does not happen by default. In Africa, this means specifically testing for performance gaps across languages, regions, and demographics.

02

Transparency — Know What the System Is Doing and Why

Black-box AI — systems that give outputs with no explanation — is unacceptable for high-stakes decisions. Affected individuals have a right to know when AI influenced a decision about them, and to understand why. "The algorithm decided" is not an acceptable answer in healthcare, justice, or employment.

03

Accountability — Humans Must Remain Responsible

When an AI system causes harm, a human being must be accountable. This means maintaining clear governance structures, audit trails, and human oversight — particularly for decisions that affect rights, safety, or opportunities. "The AI did it" is never sufficient.

04

Privacy — Data Is a Human Right, Not a Resource

AI systems are voracious data consumers. Every interaction, search, and transaction creates data points that train and influence systems. Individuals must have informed consent, meaningful control, and the right to be forgotten. This is especially critical where regulatory frameworks are still developing.

05

Safety & Reliability — If It Can Fail, Plan for It

AI systems fail — sometimes catastrophically. An AI-assisted medical diagnostic tool that is 94% accurate sounds impressive until you're the 6%. High-stakes systems must be tested rigorously, deployed with human review, and monitored continuously. There must always be a fallback.

06

Inclusivity — Designed For All, Not Just Some

If an AI system works brilliantly in English and poorly in Setswana, it is not an inclusive system. If it requires a smartphone and reliable internet to function, it excludes the majority of Africans. Ethical AI actively considers the needs and contexts of underrepresented and underserved populations.

03 · The Bias Problem

Algorithmic Bias: Hidden, Harmful, Scalable

Bias in AI is not an accident. It is an inheritance — from the biased data, biased teams, and biased assumptions baked into every system.

"Garbage in, garbage out — but at the speed of light and with the authority of a machine."
— The core problem with biased training data

🔍 Where Bias Comes From

Training Data Bias — Most AI is trained on historical data that reflects historical discrimination. A hiring AI trained on 20 years of hires will replicate 20 years of biases.
Representation Bias — Datasets massively over-represent certain demographics. Facial recognition trained mostly on light-skinned faces performs poorly on dark-skinned faces — a documented, dangerous failure.
Team Bias — If the team building the AI is not diverse, blind spots go unnoticed. Homogeneous teams build homogeneous products.
Measurement Bias — What gets measured shapes what gets optimised. If success metrics don't account for equity, AI will optimise for efficiency while creating disparate harm.
04 · Real Cases

When AI Ethics Fails: The Evidence

These are documented cases, not hypotheticals. Learn them — then watch for the same patterns in your context.

Facial Recognition

🇺🇸 The NIST Study — Error Rates by Race

The US National Institute of Standards tested 189 facial recognition algorithms across demographic groups. The findings were unambiguous:

Finding: False positive rates for African-American and Asian faces were 10–100 times higher than for Caucasian faces in many algorithms. Meaning: people of colour were far more likely to be misidentified — as potential criminals, security threats, or the wrong person entirely. These same systems are now deployed in policing, airport security, and border control globally — including in Africa.
Healthcare AI

🏥 The Pulse Oximeter Problem

Pulse oximeters — devices that measure blood oxygen levels — were calibrated primarily on light-skinned patients. During the COVID-19 pandemic, research showed that:

Finding: Pulse oximeters overestimated blood oxygen levels in patients with darker skin, leading to delayed treatment and worse outcomes. An AI-assisted triage system trained on oximeter data inherited this bias. Patients who appeared well based on AI analysis were actually deteriorating. This is healthcare bias that costs lives — and it was invisible until researchers specifically looked for it.
Hiring AI · Africa-Relevant

🌍 The CV Screening Problem Across Africa

International organisations operating in Africa are increasingly using AI CV screeners to filter applicants. These systems were typically trained on successful hire data from North America and Europe.

What happens: Universities in Botswana, Ghana, Kenya, or Nigeria that the AI "doesn't recognise" score lower. Names that don't match Western phonetic patterns may be flagged. Gaps in employment that are normal in contexts with different economic realities are penalised. Skilled, qualified African professionals are filtered out before a human ever reviews their application — because the AI was trained on a different definition of success.
⚠️

The Scale Problem

Human bias harms individuals. Algorithmic bias — running at the speed of software, applied to millions of decisions simultaneously — harms populations. A biased hiring manager rejects a few people. A biased AI hiring tool rejects thousands before anyone notices. This is why ethics cannot wait until after deployment.

05 · African Context

AI Ethics in Africa: Specific Risks and Opportunities

Africa needs its own AI ethics conversation — not one imported wholesale from Silicon Valley or Brussels.

⚠️ Specific Risks for Africa

  • Digital colonialism — AI systems built elsewhere, deployed here, without African input or consent
  • Surveillance infrastructure — AI-powered surveillance technology sold to governments without adequate accountability frameworks
  • Language exclusion — Over 2,000 African languages. Most AI: English-first, everything else a distant second
  • Data extraction — African user data training models that benefit companies elsewhere
  • Weak regulatory frameworks — Most African countries are still developing AI governance — the gap between deployment and oversight is dangerously wide

✅ Unique African Opportunities

  • Leapfrog ethics — Building AI governance frameworks from scratch, without legacy systems to protect
  • Ubuntu AI ethics — A relational, community-centred ethics framework that could be more powerful than Western individualist approaches
  • Problem-relevant training data — African developers building AI on African data for African problems
  • Youth dividend — Africa's youngest population in the world, if equipped with AI literacy, could lead the next wave
  • Mobile-first innovation — WhatsApp, USSD, and mobile-native AI solutions built for how Africans actually use technology
06 · The Checklist

The Ethics Review Checklist

Use this before deploying, recommending, or endorsing any AI system. If you can't answer these questions, the system is not ready.

Who was this built for? Who was left out?
Name the specific populations whose needs were considered in design — and those who were not.
Has it been tested for bias across demographics?
Including race, gender, age, language, and economic status. "We checked for bias" is not the same as "we tested across specific groups."
Is there a human in the loop for consequential decisions?
Any decision affecting a person's rights, safety, opportunities, or dignity requires human review. Non-negotiable.
Do users know AI is involved?
Transparency is not optional. People have a right to know when AI is influencing decisions about them.
What happens when it goes wrong?
Who is accountable? What is the remedy process for someone harmed by an incorrect AI output?
How is personal data collected, stored, and used?
Data minimisation, consent, and the right to deletion are rights — not features.
Could it be misused? Have we planned for that?
Adversarial use cases must be considered before deployment. What happens if someone deliberately tries to exploit this system?
Exercise 01 · Analysis

🔬 The Ethics Audit

Choose an AI tool you currently use or encounter — a job platform, a government service, a health app, a social media algorithm. Apply the 6 Principles of Responsible AI to it.

  1. Name the tool and describe what it does
  2. For each of the 6 principles: how does the tool perform? Rate it 1–5.
  3. Identify the single biggest ethical concern
  4. Propose one concrete improvement

Exercise 02 · Group Debate

⚖️ The Trolley Problem — AI Version

A self-driving car must choose: swerve and potentially harm one pedestrian, or brake and potentially harm its passenger. Who should the AI prioritise? Who decides?

Extended for Africa context: An AI triage system in an understaffed rural hospital must allocate the one available ICU bed. It scores patients by "survival probability." An elder with lower survival probability is scored below a younger patient. How should cultural values around elder respect inform this design decision?

  • What should the AI do?
  • Who should make that design decision?
  • Should communities affected by the AI have input in its design?
  • What does your answer reveal about whose values are built into AI by default?
